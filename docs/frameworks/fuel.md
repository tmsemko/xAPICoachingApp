# FUEL — Frame, Understand, Explore, Lay Out a Plan

## Classification

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Primary job function** | Sales coaching & frontline manager coaching |
| **Secondary job functions** | Performance management, feedback conversations, call/deal debriefs, onboarding coaching |
| **Coaching style** | Structured, conversation-framing, feedback-friendly |
| **Origin** | John Zenger & Kathleen Stinnett, *The Extraordinary Coach* (2010); widely adopted in sales enablement and corporate manager-as-coach programs |
| **Typical session length** | 10–45 minutes; built for busy managers and pipeline/deal reviews |

## Executive Summary

FUEL was designed by Zenger Folkman to fix the way managers *actually* coach: they skip the setup, assume they understand the situation, and jump straight to advice. FUEL forces the two steps managers most often skip — **Framing** the conversation (purpose, process, outcome agreed upfront) and **Understanding** the coachee's view before offering any of the manager's own. It then **Explores** the desired state and **Lays out a success plan** with explicit follow-up. Because the framing step handles "why we're talking," FUEL is exceptionally good for coach-initiated conversations — performance feedback, sales call debriefs, deal reviews — where GROW's coachee-led opening feels awkward. Sales organizations adopted it broadly because it maps cleanly onto pipeline and skill coaching cadences. Its structure is directive-friendly, which is a strength for accountability conversations but makes it less suited to deep developmental or transformational coaching.

## The Framework

### F — Frame the Conversation
Set the context: agree the purpose, the process, and the desired end point of the conversation. This is what legitimizes a manager-initiated coaching moment.

Key questions and moves:
- "I'd like to talk about the Meridian deal — my goal is to help you think through next steps. Does that work?"
- What outcome would make this conversation useful for you?
- Here's how I'd like to spend the time — what would you add?
- Agree the behavior/skill in focus (one topic, not five).

### U — Understand the Current State
Explore the coachee's point of view *first* and withhold your own read until theirs is fully on the table. This is where most managers fail.

Key questions:
- How do you see the situation? Walk me through what happened.
- What's working? What's getting in the way?
- How do you think the customer/team experienced that?
- What impact is this having — on results, on you, on others?
- (Only after their view is complete) May I share what I observed?

### E — Explore the Desired State
Define what success looks like and generate paths to it before picking one.

Key questions:
- What would you like to see happen instead? What does great look like?
- What would hitting quota / nailing that demo / a smooth launch require?
- What are two or three ways you could get there?
- What are the trade-offs of each path? Which fits you best?

### L — Lay Out a Success Plan
Convert the chosen path into specific actions, milestones, support, and a follow-up date.

Key questions:
- What exactly will you do, and by when?
- What's the very first step this week?
- What obstacles do you anticipate, and how will you handle them?
- What support do you need from me? When should we check progress?

## Rubric

*The Extraordinary Coach* ships with self-assessment instruments; the operational rubrics below are the standard adaptations used in sales enablement and manager-as-coach programs built on FUEL.

### FUEL Conversation Rubric (score each row 1–4)

| Dimension | 1 — Developing | 2 — Emerging | 3 — Proficient | 4 — Exemplary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Framing** | Launches into feedback/advice with no setup | Topic stated, but no purpose or process agreed | Purpose, process, and outcome agreed; single focus | Coachee co-owns the agenda; psychological safety explicitly built |
| **Understanding** | Manager states their view first and only | Token question, then manager's assessment dominates | Coachee's full view surfaced before manager shares observations | Coachee self-identifies the gap; manager's observations offered by permission and land as additive |
| **Exploring desired state** | Manager prescribes the fix | One path considered — usually the manager's | Success vividly defined; 2–3 alternative paths weighed | Coachee selects the path and articulates why it beats the alternatives |
| **Success plan** | "Let's touch base sometime" | Action agreed, no date or measure | Actions, dates, obstacles, and support defined | Plan includes milestones, leading indicators, and a booked follow-up |
| **Talk ratio / advice discipline** | Manager talks 70%+ | Manager ~50%, frequent unsolicited advice | Coachee talks 65%+; advice by permission | Coachee talks 75%+; manager's few contributions are high-leverage questions |

**Scoring guide:** 17–20 extraordinary coach · 13–16 proficient · 9–12 telling, not coaching · 5–8 feedback monologue.

### Sales Coaching Application Rubric (deal/call debrief variant)

| Stage | What good looks like in a sales debrief |
|---|---|
| **Frame** | Rep knows which call/deal and which *one skill* (e.g., discovery questions) is in focus before the session |
| **Understand** | Rep self-assesses the call first; manager's observations reference specific moments, not generalities |
| **Explore** | Desired state tied to a sales metric (conversion, stage velocity, ACV); rep generates alternative approaches |
| **Lay out** | Behavioral commitment applied to a *named* upcoming call/deal, with manager listening in or reviewing the recording as follow-up |

## Best Use Case

**Sales managers running recurring call debriefs, deal reviews, and performance conversations — and any manager who must *initiate* a coaching conversation the employee didn't ask for.** The Frame step turns feedback into a legitimate two-way conversation, and the success-plan step ties directly into pipeline cadence and QBR follow-up. Choose GROW when the coachee brings the topic, and CLEAR when the issue is relational or runs deeper than skill and behavior.
