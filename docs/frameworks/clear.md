# CLEAR — Contracting, Listening, Exploring, Action, Review

## Classification

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Primary job function** | Executive & senior leadership coaching |
| **Secondary job functions** | Management coaching, HR/people-partner conversations, conflict and change coaching |
| **Coaching style** | Transformational, relational |
| **Origin** | Professor Peter Hawkins (early 1980s, predates GROW's publication) |
| **Typical session length** | 45–90 minutes; designed for recurring engagements |

## Executive Summary

CLEAR was developed by Peter Hawkins for transformational coaching — changing how a leader *thinks and feels*, not just what they *do*. It wraps the working phases of a session inside two disciplines GROW lacks: an explicit **Contract** at the start (what the coachee wants, how they want to be coached, what's in/out of scope) and a **Review** at the end (feedback on the session itself). Its center of gravity is **Listening** and **Exploring** — the coach works with emotions, patterns, and underlying beliefs rather than racing to an action plan. CLEAR is the framework of choice for executive coaching, sensitive HR conversations, and situations where the presenting problem is rarely the real problem. It demands more coach skill and more time than GROW, making it heavier than needed for routine operational coaching.

## The Framework

### C — Contracting
Open by agreeing the scope, desired outcome, and ground rules for the conversation.

Key questions:
- What do you want to focus on today, and what would make this hour valuable?
- How do you want me to be with you — challenging, supportive, sounding board?
- What's in scope and out of scope for this conversation?
- How will we know we've succeeded by the end?

### L — Listening
Active, generative listening — the coach reflects, paraphrases, and attends to emotion and energy, helping the coachee hear themselves.

Key behaviors:
- Reflect back exact words and notice charged language
- Name what you observe: "Your energy dropped when you mentioned the board."
- Allow silence; resist filling gaps
- Listen for the person, not just the problem

### E — Exploring
Two movements: (1) help the coachee understand the personal impact the situation is having on them; (2) challenge them to generate possibilities for the future.

Key questions:
- What impact is this having on you? On others around you?
- What patterns do you notice — where has this shown up before?
- What belief would you have to let go of for this to change?
- What becomes possible if that changes?

### A — Action
The coachee chooses a route forward and, distinctively, often **rehearses it live in the room** ("fast-forward rehearsal") so the coach can give feedback on the actual behavior.

Key questions:
- Of everything we've explored, what will you act on?
- What's the first step, and when will you take it?
- Let's rehearse it — say it to me as if I were them.
- What will you do if you hit resistance?

### R — Review
Close by reviewing both the actions committed and the coaching process itself — building the coachee's ownership and improving the coaching relationship.

Key questions:
- What are you taking away from this session?
- What worked about how we worked together? What should we do differently?
- How will you hold yourself accountable, and what should I follow up on?

## Rubric

Hawkins pairs CLEAR with his "four levels of engagement" — a built-in depth rubric for judging where a coaching conversation is operating. A session evaluation rubric used in CLEAR-based supervision is also included.

### Depth of Engagement Scale (Hawkins)

| Level | Focus | Indicator in session |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Facts** | Data, events, story-telling | Coachee narrates what happened |
| **2. Behaviors / patterns** | Recurring actions | Coachee recognizes "I always do this when..." |
| **3. Feelings / emotions** | Personal impact | Coachee expresses how it lands on them |
| **4. Assumptions / mindset** | Beliefs driving the pattern | Coachee questions the belief that creates the behavior |

Transformational coaching requires reaching at least level 3–4; a session that stays at level 1–2 is advisory, not transformational.

### CLEAR Session Rubric (score each row 1–4)

| Dimension | 1 — Developing | 2 — Emerging | 3 — Proficient | 4 — Exemplary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Contracting** | No contract; coach assumes the agenda | Topic agreed, but no outcome or process contract | Outcome, scope, and coaching style agreed upfront | Contract revisited mid-session when the real issue emerges |
| **Listening depth** | Coach interrupts, problem-solves | Accurate paraphrase of content | Reflects emotion and energy shifts, not just words | Coachee reports hearing themselves differently; level 3–4 reached |
| **Exploring** | Stays on surface facts | Explores situation but not personal impact | Impact and patterns surfaced | Underlying assumption named and challenged; new possibility generated |
| **Action & rehearsal** | No action | Action stated but untested | Specific action with first step and timing | Action rehearsed live with feedback; contingency discussed |
| **Review** | Session just ends | Actions summarized | Learning + actions reviewed; coachee self-assesses | Coachee gives feedback on the coaching; process adjustments agreed |

**Scoring guide:** 17–20 transformational · 13–16 solid coaching, push for depth · 9–12 transactional · 5–8 advisory conversation, not coaching.

## Best Use Case

**Executive coaching engagements and high-stakes leadership conversations where the presenting issue masks a deeper pattern** — e.g., a senior leader whose "time management problem" is really a belief about control, or a sensitive performance conversation an HR partner must contract carefully. The contracting and review phases also make CLEAR the safest framework for coaching across power differences. For quick operational coaching, GROW or FUEL is faster and sufficient.
