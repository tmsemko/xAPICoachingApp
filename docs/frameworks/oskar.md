# OSKAR — Outcome, Scaling, Know-how, Affirm & Action, Review

## Classification

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Primary job function** | Management / team performance coaching |
| **Secondary job functions** | Project & agile team coaching, customer success coaching, morale and change situations |
| **Coaching style** | Solution-focused (strengths-based) |
| **Origin** | Mark McKergow & Paul Z. Jackson, *The Solutions Focus* (2002); roots in solution-focused brief therapy (de Shazer & Berg) |
| **Typical session length** | 15–45 minutes; explicitly designed for brief, repeatable conversations |

## Executive Summary

OSKAR is the leading *solution-focused* coaching framework: instead of analyzing the problem and its causes, it directs nearly all attention to what the desired future looks like and what is **already working**. Its signature move is the built-in **Scaling** question — "On a scale of 0–10, where are you now?" — followed not by "why so low?" but "why so high? what's already getting you to a 4?" This makes OSKAR unusually fast, energizing, and resistant to blame spirals, which is why it thrives in team settings, retrospectives, and organizations fatigued by problem-analysis. The **Affirm** step (evidence-based positive feedback) builds confidence alongside capability. Its limitation is the flip side of its strength: it deliberately skips root-cause analysis, so it's the wrong tool when a problem genuinely needs diagnosis (e.g., a technical failure or a compliance issue).

## The Framework

### O — Outcome
Establish the desired future state — not the problem to remove, but the outcome to create.

Key questions:
- What would you like to have happen?
- Suppose the problem vanished overnight — what's the first thing you'd notice tomorrow? (the "miracle question")
- What difference would that make to you and your team?
- What's your objective for the next 20 minutes?

### S — Scaling
Locate today's position on a 0–10 scale toward the outcome, then mine the score for what's already working.

Key questions:
- On a scale of 0–10, with 10 = the outcome fully achieved, where are you today?
- You said 4 — what's making it a 4 and not a 0? (never "why so low")
- What would one point higher look like? What would others notice?
- Where on the scale would be good enough?

### K — Know-how
Surface the skills, resources, past successes, and people that produced the current score — the raw material for progress.

Key questions:
- What's helped you get this far?
- When has something like this gone well before? What did you do?
- Who around you has know-how you could borrow?
- What do you know about this that others don't?

### A — Affirm & Action
Affirm: reflect back concrete strengths observed in the conversation. Action: agree small next steps that reuse what already works.

Key behaviors and questions:
- Affirm with evidence: "What strikes me is how you kept the client engaged even while the project slipped."
- What's already working that you'll do more of?
- What small step will you take this week to move one point up the scale?
- What will you do differently tomorrow?

### R — Review
Open the *next* conversation by reviewing progress — OSKAR is designed as a loop, not a one-off.

Key questions:
- What's better since we last spoke?
- What did you do that made the difference?
- What effect did it have? What do you think will be different next?
- Where are you on the scale now?

## Rubric

The 0–10 scale is OSKAR's native rubric and is used directly with the coachee (unlike coach-evaluation rubrics). Both the coachee-facing scale and a coach-evaluation rubric are included.

### Coachee Scaling Rubric (used live in every session)

| Score | Meaning | Coaching move |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Outcome not present at all; no known resources | Search for exceptions: "Has there ever been a moment it was slightly better?" |
| 1–3 | Early signs; some know-how exists but unrecognized | Amplify: "What's getting you to a 3 and not a 0?" |
| 4–6 | Clear progress; platform of working behaviors | Identify the +1: "What does a 6 look like? What's the smallest step?" |
| 7–8 | Mostly achieved; sustaining is the focus | Consolidate: "What must you keep doing? Is this good enough to stop here?" |
| 9–10 | Outcome achieved | Review, affirm, and close or re-contract on a new outcome |

Track scores across sessions — the delta between Reviews is the framework's progress measure.

### OSKAR Coach Fidelity Rubric (score each row 1–4)

| Dimension | 1 — Developing | 2 — Emerging | 3 — Proficient | 4 — Exemplary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Outcome focus** | Conversation centers on the problem and its causes | Outcome named but discussion drifts back to problem-talk | Future state described concretely | Coachee describes observable "day after the miracle" detail |
| **Scaling** | No scale used | Scale asked but score left unexplored | "What makes it an N, not zero?" mined for working elements | Scale reused for progress ("good enough" level agreed, +1 step defined) |
| **Know-how & resources** | Coach supplies the answers | Generic strengths mentioned | Specific past successes and transferable skills surfaced | Counters (people, platforms, prior wins) mapped and reused in the action |
| **Affirmation** | None, or empty praise | Generic compliment ("great job") | Affirmation tied to specific observed evidence | Affirmation visibly shifts coachee energy/confidence; coachee self-affirms |
| **Small actions & review loop** | No action | Large, vague action | Small step scoped to move +1 on the scale | Step committed, and next session opens with "what's better?" review |

**Scoring guide:** 17–20 exemplary solution-focused practice · 13–16 proficient · 9–12 problem-focus creeping in · 5–8 retrain on solution-focused fundamentals.

## Best Use Case

**Team and performance coaching where energy is low and problem-analysis has hit diminishing returns** — sprint retrospectives, customer-success and support team huddles, change fatigue, or a capable performer stuck in a confidence dip. The scaling loop gives managers a lightweight, measurable cadence (weekly 15-minute check-ins with a visible score trend). Avoid it for issues that genuinely require root-cause diagnosis or compliance investigation.
